The Pinedale Aquatic Center hopes to keep its doors open.¹ The $17 million Wyoming facility was built during times flush with oil and gas revenue, back when money felt permanent. Now those revenues have cratered and the town’s working to keep the lights on at a swimming pool.

Meanwhile, in Everett, Washington, when Boeing’s machinists went on strike for seven weeks in the fall of 2024, local car dealerships watched their sales plummet. Restaurant owners in Renton saw fewer customers. The entire regional economy held its breath. One aerospace job supports roughly five other jobs in the local economy, so when Boeing sneezes, Western Washington catches pneumonia.2

## The Export Clock: When Your Fortune Depends on What Happens Elsewhere

Washington State’s economy rises and falls with Boeing. The company employs around 66,000 people directly, generates over $71 billion in business revenues, and accounts for roughly 80% of the state’s aerospace jobs.5 Aerospace exports alone made up 53% of all Washington exports in 2013, totaling over $43 billion.6

That’s what export-driven growth looks like: high wages, high productivity, and high anxiety. The average aerospace worker in Washington earns $133,200—well above the state average.7 Those workers then spend their paychecks at local restaurants, car dealerships, and grocery stores. In Auburn and Renton, aerospace employees were responsible for 2.4% of restaurant sales in 2023, directly supporting 420 restaurant jobs.8

But, the catch: when Boeing’s order book shrinks, or when there’s a strike, or when the company decides to move production to South Carolina, those restaurant jobs disappear too. Global airlines drive demand for 787s, and every few years, Washington gets reminded that its prosperity is tied to decisions made in boardrooms in Singapore and Dubai.

This is the fundamental tension of export-led growth. It works awesomely when it works. Washington consistently ranks near number one nationally in labor productivity, aerospace sales and exports, and aerospace engineers.9 The state has produced over 33,000 commercial and military aircraft over the past century.10 But the success is fragile: the economy relies on what other people buy, and other people can always decide to buy less.

## The Migration Clock: Growth as a Feedback Loop

Utah’s economy doesn’t work like Washington’s. It’s not dominated by one massive exporter. Instead, Utah has been growing through a different mechanism: people show up, which creates jobs, which attracts more people.

The state added 50,392 residents in 2024. That’s roughly the size of Murray City appearing in one year.11 Those new residents need housing, which creates construction jobs. They need healthcare, retail, schools, and local services. Each new household generates its own economic multiplier. The tech sector alone generated $22.5 billion in economic impact in 2023—10% of Utah’s entire economy.12

This model scales fast. Utah’s net tech employment increased 5% in 2024, and the state ranks first nationally for projected tech job growth from 2024 to 2034, at 33%.13 Since 2000, growth in engineering employment in Utah has outpaced the rest of the US by more than fivefold.14

But, another catch: Utah County added 21,853 new residents last year—43% of the state’s total growth.15 Home prices are up 6.2% year-over-year, reaching a median of $559,400.16 The state now has the 7th-highest median home prices in the country. Lehi has seen 12% population growth since 2020, and infrastructure is straining. Traffic is worse. Water is scarcer. Housing is expensive.

The migration clock works until it doesn’t. When housing costs rise faster than wages, when commutes get too long, when schools get overcrowded, people stop coming. Or they come anyway and drive up costs until locals can’t afford to stay. Utah’s net migration peaked in 2021-2022 during COVID and has been dropping since. The state gained 19,223 people from migration in 2025, down from over 30,000 annually from 2021-2023.17 The challenge isn’t keeping growth going for another year. It’s keeping growth sustainable for another decade.

## The Resource Clock: Boom, Bust, Repeat

Wyoming’s economy runs on a different schedule. Oil and gas provided over $11 billion to the state over six years. In 2023 alone, the industry’s contributions equated to roughly $4,143 per Wyoming resident.18 In fiscal year 2024, severance taxes from oil were more than triple what came from coal.19

This is growth that arrives in waves. When energy prices spike, revenue floods in. The state funds schools, builds infrastructure, cuts taxes. When prices crash—or when coal demand collapses, or when natural gas prices crater—everything reverses. Wyoming produced nearly 200 million tons of coal in good years; in 2024 it struggled to hit that mark for the first time in 30 years.

The brutal math of resource dependence means Wyoming can’t control the cycle. Venezuelan oil coming to Western markets could impact Wyoming’s budget. A warm winter reduces natural gas demand. New renewable capacity displaces coal plants. Smart resource states build rainy day funds—Wyoming’s is approaching $2 billion. They forecast revenues conservatively. They try to diversify. But diversification is hard when more than half your state budget comes from taxing fossil fuels, and when extracting those fuels provides the high-wage jobs that keep people in the state.

That’s the resource clock: you’re playing a game where the rules keep changing and you’re not the one changing them.

## One Region, Three Rhythms

Most Western states have some combination of these three growth models. Colorado has aerospace and defense (export clock), massive in-migration to Denver and Boulder (migration clock), and energy production on the Western Slope (resource clock). Arizona has tech clusters, retirement migration, and copper mining. Nevada has tourism exports, California migration, and... well, mostly California migration pricing everyone out.

The insight is that each state runs on fundamentally unique combination of economic rhythms, and those rhythms create different vulnerabilities.

Export economies are fragile to global demand shocks. When China slows down, Washington’s aerospace orders drop. When recession hits, companies delay aircraft purchases for years.

Migration economies are fragile to their own success. The more people who come for affordability and opportunity, the less affordable and full of opportunity the place becomes. Ask anyone who moved to Boise in 2019.

Resource economies are fragile to commodity cycles and technological disruption. Wyoming can’t legislate higher oil prices or make utilities buy more coal.

There’s no perfect model. Export-led growth creates prosperity but ties your fate to global markets. Migration-led growth works until housing costs spiral. Resource-led growth produces windfalls but guarantees busts.

The states that thrive understand which clock they’re on and plan to dance accordingly. Washington needs to keep investing in workforce development and infrastructure so it doesn’t lose aerospace jobs to other states. Utah needs to build housing and transportation infrastructure faster than people arrive. Wyoming needs fiscal buffers and economic diversification so the next oil crash doesn’t devastate public services.

What drives growth in the West? The answer of course: it depends which clock in the West you’re talking about. And more importantly, it depends whether anyone’s paying attention to what time it is.
