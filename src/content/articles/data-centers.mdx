We’re long past being naive about data centers. 

We now know they claim power from the grid, water from a basin, and land that could have served another use. They’re a noise source, a heat source, and a source of stress for local governments. It claims room in transmission plans, permits from air and water regulators, time from county staff, and future options the next employer, hospital, or neighborhood may need. 

Some of those claims are easy to price, but a lot of them aren’t. The data-center boom has become so large that the gap between what companies pay and what communities absorb has become a policy problem.

The Western U.S. is never one to miss a boom. The Western Electricity Coordinating Council (WECC), which monitors reliability across the Western grid, expects demand will rise by 192 terawatt-hours from 2025 to 2034. For scale, that increase is larger than all electricity used by U.S. data centers in 2023. Data centers will not drive all of that Western growth, but they are one of the clearest signs that the region’s long period of nearly stagnant electricity demand is ending.

Despite the obvious costs of this data center build out, Western communities are currently pricing only one piece of what large-load projects consume: the electric bill. Instead, the right answer would be to price all of it — power, water, land, air quality, noise, tax risk, infrastructure risk, and the value of keeping future options open.

One mechanism for doing this would be a public local-capacity ledger, required before any major project receives final approval. No ledger, no approval.

The rule wouldn’t apply only to data centers. Semiconductor fabs, battery plants, hydrogen projects, crypto mines, mineral processing facilities, and other extraordinary loads can create the same basic problem. The West really needs a large-load policy rather than just a data center policy: ordinary customers should receive ordinary service; extraordinary loads should face extraordinary scrutiny.

# From Abundance to Allocation

For a long time, the West could treat electricity as an advantage. Cheap hydropower, open land, cool nights, dry air, strong fiber routes, and willing local governments made the region attractive to data centers long before artificial intelligence became the center of the national conversation. That bargain rested on a dangerous assumption: that the next large user could be added without forcing hard choices. Well, we now know that assumption no longer holds true.

The Western Interconnection is the synchronized power grid linking most of the western United States with parts of Canada and Mexico. In 2024, it reached 926 terawatt-hours of annual demand (or 86 million homes annual electricity). This all-time high was up 3.7 percent from the prior year — far faster than the region's average growth over the previous decade. 

Increasingly, that growth is arriving in large, concentrated increments. A single 100-megawatt facility operating continuously would consume nearly one terawatt-hour a year, and proposed campuses can be several times larger. Utilities must absorb at one site, and often on a compressed timetable. Previously demand accumulated gradually across whole communities.

These large-loads change the decision-making process around customer additions. It’s now an allocation decision — a choice about who gets what from a system that can no longer say yes to everyone at once.

Four things make this distinctly Western. Water has a sharper shadow price here: drought risk, aquifer stress, Colorado River politics, and tribal water rights influence the cost of every gallon. The Western grid covers vast distances with long transmission corridors and fast-growing load pockets, making new infrastructure expensive and slow. Much of the region's power comes from public utilities built around a promise to serve local people at fair rates, not to become bargain infrastructure for private compute. And the West has lived through enough resource booms to recognize the pattern: outside capital typically prices the scarce thing before local governments do.

# Let me see the full price

Let’s talk billing. No one is wondering whether or not data centers will pay for the electricity they use (they will)—but does the price they pay cover the cost of changing the system? 

Think of the difference between average cost and what we might call next cost. Average cost reflects the expense of the existing power system spread across all customers. Next cost is the expense of adding the next large load: new transmission lines, substations, firm generating capacity, or long-term power contracts. In a system with little spare capacity, next cost can be substantially higher than the average embedded in existing rates.

If a data center pays something close to average cost while requiring infrastructure built at next cost, someone must cover the difference. Unless the project’s rate and contract assign those costs to the project, they may appear in higher rates for households and small businesses, delayed connections for other customers, or infrastructure that remains in the utility’s rate base even if the anticipated demand never arrives.

There is a second pricing problem before a facility uses any electricity: its place in line. Developers can request hundreds of megawatts years before they are ready to build, leaving utilities and regulators unsure how much proposed demand will become real. Those requests can still shape transmission studies, substation plans, generation forecasts, and the amount of capacity considered available for other customers.

A claim on scarce grid capacity should therefore require a meaningful financial commitment. Deposits or collateral should rise with the size of the request. Large customers should face minimum monthly bills, long-term contracts, exit fees, and direct responsibility for project-specific upgrades. Utilities should also report requested, contracted, and energized load separately. Otherwise, speculative projects can influence billions of dollars in planning while putting little of their own money at risk.

# Flexiblity Should Change the Price

The amount of power a data center requests is only part of its cost to the grid. Timing matters too.

Some computing work must happen immediately, including payment processing, communications, and other latency-sensitive services. Other workloads—such as AI training, batch processing, video encoding, and backups—can sometimes be shifted by minutes or hours. A facility designed to move that work away from periods of grid stress is less costly to serve than one that requires its full allocation every hour of the year.

Rates should reflect that distinction. A rigid data center should pay for the generation and network capacity needed to guarantee firm service at peak demand. A flexible facility should receive lower capacity charges or compensation only to the extent that it can reliably reduce its draw when the grid is tight.

That flexibility must be measurable and enforceable. The contract should specify how many megawatts can be curtailed, how quickly the reduction must occur, how long it can last, how often it can be called, and what happens if the customer fails to deliver. Batteries, thermal storage, and backup generation should count only when they produce a verified reduction in demand on the grid.

The best large-load tariff will therefore ask two questions: how much power does the project want, and how much must the utility guarantee during the system’s most constrained hours? The second number determines much of the infrastructure the rest of the system must build and maintain.

# Water, Land, Air, and Tax: The Rest of the Ledger

Power is the easiest line to see because utilities already meter it. The rest of the bargain is harder to see, which makes it easier to give away.

Start with water. National averages tell a Western community very little. The relevant question is not how much water data centers use across the country, but where a particular facility gets its water and what else depends on that source. Reclaimed wastewater is different from treated drinking water. A secure surface-water supply is different from an aquifer already being drawn down.

Cooling choices also move pressure between water and power. Evaporative cooling can reduce electricity use while consuming more water. Dry cooling conserves water but can require more electricity during the hottest part of the day, when the grid is already strained. A serious review has to evaluate both sides of that tradeoff.

Consider a facility using 100 million gallons a year, or about 307 acre-feet. At a relatively low scarcity benchmark of $500 per acre-foot, that annual use carries an implied value of about $153,000. At $2,000 per acre-foot, it is roughly $614,000. Secure, long-term access can be worth much more: one Arizona municipal transaction implies a capital value of about $3.6 million for an entitlement to that annual quantity. These are different kinds of benchmarks, not three universal prices. Together, they show why the posted water rate may capture the cost of treatment and delivery while missing the value of the underlying resource.

Land deserves equal honesty. Data centers can occupy large, infrastructure-ready sites while supporting relatively few permanent workers once construction ends. That alone does not make them a poor use of land. It does mean local officials should compare the project with realistic alternatives for that parcel: another employer, housing, a public use, or simply keeping the option to decide later. Once a large campus is built, that choice may be difficult to reverse for decades.

Tax incentives are another commitment from the public side of the ledger. The basic test is whether the incentive changed the company’s decision—the “but for” question economists have studied for decades. Would the project have come without the subsidy? If so, the community may be giving up revenue without buying much additional investment. Any agreement should disclose the full value of the incentive, state what the public is receiving in return, and include clawbacks if promised investment or employment does not arrive.

Noise, lighting, backup-generator emissions, and truck traffic belong in the ledger too. These impacts may be manageable, and in many cases they will be. But standards should be set before construction, with monitoring, public reporting, and enforcement after the facility begins operating. Communities should not have to discover the terms of the bargain after the project is already built.

By the time a project reaches a final vote, residents should be able to see the whole local bargain in one place: what the project will consume, what it will pay, what it is required to deliver, and who carries the risk if those commitments fall short.

# No Ledger, No Final Approval

A good data-center deal is possible. It starts with a site suited to heavy industry and enough existing grid capacity—or a fully funded plan to add it—so the connection does not delay or overcharge other customers. The developer puts real money behind its place in line, pays for project-specific upgrades, and signs a long-term contract with minimum bills and exit fees. Its water source and cooling plan fit the basin. Any promise to reduce demand during grid stress is measurable, enforceable, and backed by penalties. Any tax incentive buys a result the community would not otherwise receive.

Under those terms, a data center can bring investment and tax revenue while keeping most of the downside with the company creating it. Weak deals push that risk outward. They reserve scarce capacity with little money at stake, demand firm power at all hours, draw drinking water from a stressed supply, seek tax exemptions without a credible “but for” case, and leave other customers exposed if the project shrinks, stalls, or walks away. Those deals should be repriced, renegotiated, or rejected.

The local-capacity ledger is how a community tells the difference. Before the final public vote, one document should show the project’s requested and contracted electricity load; its firm peak requirement and curtailment terms; the grid upgrades it triggers and who pays for them; its water source, annual use, and drought plan; the land and local impacts involved; the full value of any tax incentive; the promised jobs and investment; and the penalties if those commitments are missed.

State law should require the ledger for any project large enough to materially alter a utility’s resource plan, its transmission plans, or a community’s water budget. The threshold should scale with the local system: a project would trigger the rule when it exceeds either a state-set megawatt floor or a specified share of the utility’s peak load. A large project can be manageable for one utility and transformative for another.

The city or county approving the site should publish the ledger, with the utility, water provider, air regulator, and development authority responsible for supplying and certifying their portions. It should be released before approval, updated whenever the project materially changes, and written so residents can understand the bargain without reading hundreds of pages of utility filings and development agreements.

No ledger, no final approval. The rule changes the order of operations: disclose the claim on local capacity, assign the costs and risks, then decide whether the project is worth it. Companies can still build, and Western communities can still compete for their investment. The terms would no longer be scattered across rate cases, water contracts, tax agreements, and private promises.

The West has seen this pattern before. Outside capital moves quickly when a local resource becomes newly valuable; public institutions usually take longer to learn its price. Data centers are cleaner at the fence line than many earlier booms, but they still draw on a finite stock of power, water, land, infrastructure, and public attention.

The scarce asset is a place’s ability to absorb growth without leaving existing residents worse off. Projects that pay their full costs, fit the basin, control their local impacts, and put real money behind their commitments should be welcome. Projects that cannot meet that standard should not receive final approval.

## A Note on Sources

The electricity demand figures in this piece come from Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory's 2024 United States Data Center Energy Usage Report and the Western Electricity Coordinating Council's State of the Interconnection 2025 and Western Assessment of Resource Adequacy. The LBNL 2028 demand range of 325 to 580 TWh reflects a wide scenario spread, not a single forecast, and should be read as a range of plausible outcomes rather than a point projection.

The PJM $4 billion transmission cost figure comes from a Rocky Mountain Institute analysis published in November 2025. It is an advocacy estimate based on utility filings, not a regulatory determination, and is cited here for order-of-magnitude context.

Nevada data comes from the Desert Research Institute's Data Center Water and Electricity Consumption in Nevada.

Water shadow-price figures draw on Stanford Water in the West's Colorado River allocation research and publicly reported Arizona water transfer transactions. The Upper Colorado conservation payment range of roughly $400 to $500 per acre-foot and the Queen Creek municipal transfer figure above $11,000 per acre-foot represent different transaction types and should not be compared directly as substitutes.

The employment comparison of 8 to 20 jobs per acre for industrial and light manufacturing uses standard urban planning benchmarks and is intended as an order-of-magnitude illustration, not a site-specific estimate.

The "but for" incentive literature draws on Timothy Bartik's Upjohn Institute research and Slattery and Zidar's Journal of Economic Perspectives review.
