The ski patrollers in Telluride, Colorado went on strike a few days after Christmas. Almost two weeks in, local business owners and residents took to the streets to warn that the dispute was bleeding into the winter economy. The conflict was ostensibly about wages, but underneath it we saw a familiar breakdown: when the workers who keep a place running can't afford to live there. This brief examines the cost of living in the West and what drives it.

## Never uniform, western extremes

Listen to the national conversation and you'd think the West is uniformly expensive. The data shows something more specific: the West holds a disproportionate share of America's most expensive places, but it also contains plenty of affordable ones.

In 2023, California ranked as the most expensive state, with a price level 12.6% above the U.S. average. Hawaii (4th most expensive) and Washington (5th) were close behind, and then Oregon (9th). These states carry weight because they’re large (California especially) and because their housing markets set the tone for how people talk about regional affordability. But the interior West tells a different story. Several plains-adjacent and mountain states rank among the cheaper places in the country. South Dakota, North Dakota, parts of the Southwest—these places sit well below the national average.

The takeaway is that the West has a wide dispersion, with a heavy concentration at the expensive end–an asymmetry that shapes migration patterns and political conflict more than expected.

Of course, anyone who has experienced the region knows the state level can mask intrastate variation. Phoenix is not Yuma. Seattle is not Walla Walla. The West, more than most regions, splits sharply between high-demand metros and cheaper surrounding markets. Looking at metropolitan-area RPPs makes the pattern clearer: the median Western metro costs about the same as the median metro elsewhere in the country (roughly 92 on the national scale). The difference appears in the tail. The West is overrepresented among the very expensive metros, and when you include California’s coastal cities, that concentration becomes unmistakable.

# Housing to blame

The San Francisco Fed has documented that higher-price states generally experienced higher inflation from 2019 through 2022, widening the gaps further (San Fran Fed). Places that were already expensive often got more so relative to cheaper states. That dynamic helps explain why “the West is too expensive” feels truer each year, even when the typical Western place looks affordable on paper.

The BEA’s component breakdowns make the diagnosis visible. For goods, RPPs cluster near 100 across most states—tradable items don’t explain much of the geographic variation. For utilities, differences reflect climate, energy mix, and infrastructure (you can see this in Hawaii, understandably, but even Idaho). But housing stands out dramatically in high-cost places. California’s overall RPP of 112.6 is driven largely by its housing costs, which sit far above the national baseline (+58%). Same with Colorado (+30%) and Washington (+25%).

The economics here has been nailed down for quite some time. Edward Glaeser and Joseph Gyourko (2018) synthesize decades of work showing how housing supply constraints change both prices and the distributional stakes. When supply can’t keep up with demand, the gap shows up as higher prices rather than more units. Chang-Tai Hsieh and Enrico Moretti (2019) take it a bit further: constraints in high-productivity cities create large aggregate losses by limiting how many workers can access those labor markets. So a local affordability problem really becomes an efficiency problem for the broader economy.

Peter Ganong and Daniel Shoag (2017) link the decline in U.S. regional income convergence to rising housing costs in high-income places. As housing got more expensive, the net payoff to moving fell, especially for lower-skill households. Or a bit more bluntly: in expensive parts of the West, housing is the gate that determines who can live there.

# What people get in return
People don’t stay in expensive places out of stubbornness (though we can be stubborn in the West). The economics is straightforward: households think about wages, prices, and amenities. Some Western metros pay well on paper, but local prices absorb a big share of that income. The Bureau of Economic Analysis pairs price-level data with income data for this reason—to compare purchasing power across places.

Consider per capita personal income (PCPI). Nominal PCPI shows what people make on paper. RPP-adjusted PCPI shows what that income is worth after accounting for local price levels. If nominal income is high but adjusted income is much lower, the extra pay is being eaten by local costs. If the adjusted number exceeds nominal, the place is relatively cheap and income stretches further. This gap is what households experience, and it’s where the Telluride-style conflicts originate: in high-amenity, supply-constrained places, workers can be priced out even when the local economy appears to be booming.

The West also offers something harder to quantify: access to public lands, mountains, climate, recreation, and a particular quality of life. Urban economists treat these as amenities that get capitalized into wages and housing costs. David Albouy’s work formalizes how wage and housing cost differences embed both productivity and amenity value, which helps explain why some metros remain expensive even when wage differences don’t look spectacular. Some of what people get back isn’t a bigger paycheck—it’s the place itself. But when housing is the bottleneck, the place becomes a luxury good.

# What Policymakers Can Do When the Balance Breaks
The fix isn’t rocket science, but it is not sexy either: build more housing where people want to live and work. That means denser stuff near jobs—duplexes, townhomes, apartments. Speed up permits, and cut the red tape that adds years and millions in costs. Invest in infrastructure that enables infill and expansion: water systems, transit, utilities. Finally, align local incentives so communities do not block housing while relying on imported labor.

In tiny resort towns like Telluride, you might need special workforce housing programs since the land is locked up by mountains and seasonal crowds. But the core idea is the same: crank out more units, quicker.

Support for renters and exposed households can reduce hardship, but if the local supply constraint is binding, broad demand subsidies often get partly capitalized into higher rents. Effective policymakers pair rental support with more housing supply.

When housing is expensive near jobs, households substitute into commuting. That creates a second cost channel: time, transport, childcare logistics, and the fragility of long commutes. Affordability policy means transportation investment and zoning near job centers.

Another idea that keeps popping up is banning big institutional investors—like private-equity firms and Wall Street landlords—from buying single-family homes in these high-demand towns. It sounds very appealing: stop the corporate vultures from snapping up houses and pricing out locals. But the data says it wouldn’t move the needle much. These firms own only a tiny slice of the housing stock nationwide (less than 4% of rentals, even less in most resort markets), and their buying has already cratered with higher interest rates. In supply-constrained Western metros, the dominant force remains housing scarcity: when demand is strong and new construction is slow, prices adjust regardless of who the marginal buyer is. Targeted restrictions may modestly ease pressure in specific segments where investor concentration is high, but they are not substitutes for policies that expand housing supply.

#Closing
On the fourteenth day of the Telluride ski patrol strike, the workers accepted a wage deal below their asking figure. This mostly stemmed from pressure from the community who stressed the potential damage to their small, mountain economy.

The West contains plenty of affordable places. But it also holds a large share of America’s most expensive housing markets and a growing number of communities where the workforce that makes the local economy run cannot live within reach of the work.

This doesn’t have to be the case. The best evidence points to a solvable constraint: housing supply that cannot or will not respond to demand. Making more units available, combined with targeted investment and support can keep communities strong.

The Telluride experience reminds us of the important balancing act. The policy choice across the West is whether to keep places open economically, not just geographically. Else, accept a future where some views stay stunning, but the community turns exclusive, priced out for everyone but the rich.
