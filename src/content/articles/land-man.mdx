In the West, you know land is important—whether you’re a farmer, a petroleum engineer, or an ecologist. But how valuable? And why does who own Western land make deciding how to use the land particularly difficult?

Land has always sat at a peculiar spot in economic theory. Unlike labor or capital, you can’t make more of it, you can’t really move it, and you can’t say all land is the same.

So, one useful way to understand the value of Western land is to break it into three dominant sources of value: productive value, amenity value, and conservation value.

## Productive Value

Productive value is the oldest and most familiar lens. Land produces things: agriculture, grazing, timber, mining, and energy extraction. Much of Western settlement and development was organized around this logic, reinforced by federal land policies that prioritized extraction and settlement. Even today, large portions of the regional economy depend on land’s ability to produce tradable goods. In places like Wyoming and Alaska, mining alone accounts for more than 10% of state GDP. In Arizona and Idaho, farming still outpaces national averages. Productivity may be uneven, but it remains core to several rural economies.

## Amenity Value

Here, the West starts to diverge. Not all land value comes from extraction—some value comes from what land enables: open space, recreation, access to beauty. In places like Colorado, Utah, and Montana, outdoor recreation (skiing, hiking, rafting, hunting) accounts for a bigger chunk of the economy than it does elsewhere. Tourists spend money, and residents accept lower wages to live near mountains and rivers. This is “amenity value” (i.e. the pleasantness of a place), and it changes from being a productive good to a consumption good. The value of land in this sense shows up in prices: housing prices, wages, and business location decisions.

## Conservation Value

There’s a third type of value that’s easy to overlook, but in the long haul, could be most important. Big stretches of Western land are worth a lot simply because we don’t build on them or dig them up. They quietly deliver benefits like healthy ecosystems, diverse plants and animals, places of cultural or historical importance, and the option for our kids and grandkids to enjoy or use them someday. You can’t buy or sell these benefits like you could with timber or housing. They spread across society and pay off over decades. Nevertheless, they’re economically valuable all the same.

## Who Owns the Land? The man, man.
What makes the West economically distinct is that these three economic values collide in a unique institutional setting: federal ownership introduces a fundamental divergence between private incentives and social objectives. In the East, most land is private—markets do more of the sorting. Because nearly half of Western land is federally managed, market forces often take a backseat. Instead, public agencies decide who gets to graze, mine, or hike. That makes land management inherently political. Different stakeholders have different visions: one rancher’s grazing lease is another conservationist’s sage grouse habitat. And because the land is shared, these tradeoffs don’t resolve easily.

## So what can be done?
Economists point to a few straightforward principles that most agree can help balance the tough choices we face over Western land—whether it’s ranching, mining, recreation, or keeping places wild. First, clear rules on who owns what and how public lands can be used, so everyone knows the boundaries. Second, accounting for spillover effects (like how leaving a watershed untouched provides clean water downstream or how heavy use can harm wildlife habitat elsewhere). Third, acknowledging opportunity costs—choosing one use means giving up others. Fourth, that solutions work best when they draw on local knowledge through shared governance involving local groups, states, and federal agencies (think of the many rancher-led watershed efforts or collaborative land projects already succeeding in the West). Finally, we should prioritize long-term resilience, building adaptability into management so the land can handle droughts, fires, and changing needs over decades.

The hard part is about is about which of these principles weigh most heavily. Folks wary of more rules often point to lost jobs, higher costs, or government missteps, while those pushing for stronger protection stress permanent losses (like extinct species or ruined views), benefits that don’t show up in markets (clean air, wildlife, tourism dollars), and leaving options open for our kids and grandkids. All of them standard economic trade-offs, made bigger here because of the West’s huge public landholdings, rugged terrain, and mix of private and federal ownership.

Smart policy in the West measures and values all the ways this land supports us—not just what we extract (timber, minerals, grazing), but recreation that draws visitors and boosts local businesses, ecosystem services like water purification and habitat, scenic beauty that keeps communities thriving, and the deliberate choice to leave some areas untouched. Tools like conservation easements (where landowners get paid or tax breaks to limit development while keeping ranching or farming going) and payments for ecosystem services can reward land that delivers these public benefits.

In short, we all know Western land serves as the backbone of our economy and lifestyle: what we grow and mine, where we hike and hunt, and what we preserve for tomorrow. The future depends on open, honest policies that face these realities head-on, that manage the inevitable tensions, and that keep this irreplaceable landscape productive, beautiful, and sustainable for generations to come.
